助詞{じょし}（ＰＡＲＴＩＣＬＥＳ）

Particles are a key aspect of grammer in Japanese, they act as suffixes or short words that follow after a noun, verb, adjective or sentence, their range varies from indicating the function and meaning of words, to changing a sentence's tone and speaker affect.

Please note that particles can overlap catagories, and that a single kana can have different meanings as part of a different catagory

格助詞{かくじょし}（ＣＡＳＥ　ＰＡＲＴＩＣＬＥＳ）

In English, word order is used to define what grammatical role (a.k.a case) a word has. In Japanese, Case Particles are used to identify the case of the noun that comes before it, for example: subject, direct object, loaction, destination, etc.

並立助詞{へいれつじょし}（ＰＡＲＡＬＬＥＬ　ＰＡＲＴＩＣＬＥＳ）

Parallel particles are used to put nouns and noun phrases in parallel (thus the name) or in a list, and in English they mostly translate to words similar to "and" and "or".

終助詞{しゅうじょし}（ＳＥＮＴＥＮＣＥ－ＥＮＤＩＮＧ　ＰＡＲＴＩＣＬＥＳ）

Sentence-ending particles are kana placed at the end of sentences to show its force, such as emphasis, confirmation, questioning, emotion, etc.

間投助詞{かんとうじょし}（ＩＮＴＥＲＪＥＣＴＯＲＹ　ＰＡＲＴＩＣＬＥＳ）

Interjectory particles can be placed mid-sentece in order to show emphasis, confirmation, questioning, emotion, etc.

副助詞{ふくじょし}（ＡＤＶＥＲＢＩＡＬ　ＰＡＲＴＩＣＬＥＳ）

Adverbial particles act similarly to adverbs (thus the name) by modifiy words or phrases to express concepts like limits, addition, emphasis, etc.

係助詞{かかりじょし}（ＢＩＮＤＩＮＧ　ＰＡＲＴＩＣＬＥＳ）

Binding particles attach/bind to words and phrases and highlight how they should be interpreted, able to create emphasis, contrast, limitations, and control what the listener pays attention to.

接続助詞{せつぞくじょし}（ＣＯＮＪＵＮＣＴＩＶＥ　ＰＡＲＴＩＣＬＥＳ）

Conjunctive particles connect one clause to another and shows the relationship between the them, whether it is in contrast, cause or reason, time sequence, condition, etc, they work the same as conectives in English (thus the name).

準体助詞{じゅんたいじょし}（ＮＯＲＭＡＬＩＳＩＮＧ　ＰＡＲＴＩＣＬＥＳ）

Normalising particles allow you to turn something that is not a noun, into a noun, allowing you to treat actions, situations, descriptions, etc as things/objects (a.k.a nouns)
